Acceleration of a spaceship

Problem:

Setup: $S$ & $S'$ in standard configuration, S' is moving away from origo of $S$ with speed $v$. Ship has speed $u_x$ in $S$ (and no speed component in $y'$ and $z'$ directions. Let's call $u_x$ just $u$ for simplicity.

Then we have:

$$u'=\frac{u-v}{1-\frac{uv}{c^2}}$$

Instantaneous measured acceleration in $S'$:

$$\frac{du'}{d\tau}=\frac{du'}{du}\frac{du}{d\tau}$$

Focusing on $\frac{du'}{du}$:

$$\frac{du'}{du}=\frac{d}{du}\left((u-v)\left(1-\frac{uv}{c^2}\right)^{-1}\right)$$

$$=1\cdot\left(1-\frac{uv}{c^2}\right)^{-1}+\frac{u-v}{\left(1-\frac{uv}{c^2}\right)^2}\frac{v}{c}$$

$$=\frac{1-\frac{uv}{c^2}+\frac{uv}{c^2}-\frac{v^2}{c^2}}{\left(1-\frac{uv}{c^2}\right)^2}$$

$$=\frac{1-\frac{v^2}{c^2}}{\left(1-\frac{uv}{c^2}\right)^2}$$

$$=\frac{1}{\gamma_v^2\left(1-\frac{uv}{c^2}\right)^2}$$

Substitute back:

$$\frac{du'}{d\tau}=\frac{1}{\gamma_v^2\left(1-\frac{uv}{c^2}\right)^2}\frac{du}{d\tau}$$

Choose $S'$ such a way that it is an instantaneous rest frame for the spaceship at $\tau=0$. Ie $$v=u(\tau=0) \text{ & } \frac{du(\tau=0)}{d\tau}=f(\tau=0)$$

Our DE at $\tau=0$:

$$\frac{du'}{d\tau} = \frac{1}{\gamma_v^2\left(1-\frac{v^2}{c^2}\right)^2}\frac{du}{d\tau}$$ $$=\gamma_v^2\frac{du}{d\tau}$$ $$\implies f(\tau) = \gamma_v^2\frac{du}{d\tau}\implies\frac{f(\tau)}{\gamma_v^2}=\frac{du}{d\tau}$$

As parametrization, let:

$$u(\tau) = c \tanh \psi$$

Then it follows that:

$$\gamma_v^2=\frac{1}{1-\frac{v^2}{c^2}}=\frac{1}{1-\tanh^2\psi}=\frac{1}{\text{sech}^2x}=\cosh^2x$$

RHS:

$$\frac{d}{d\tau}(c \tanh\psi)=c\text{ sech}^2\psi\cdot\frac{d\psi}{d\tau}$$

LHS:

$$\frac{f(\tau)}{\gamma_v^2}=\frac{1}{\cosh^2\psi}f(\tau)=\text{sech}^2\psi\cdot f(\tau)$$

RHS=LHS, cross out $\text{sech}^2\psi$ terms, arrive at the DE:

$$c\frac{d\psi}{d\tau}=f(\tau)$$

Solution:

$$\psi(\tau)=\frac{1}{c}\int^\tau f(\tau')d\tau'$$

Noting that the problem wants us to get an integral from $0$ to $\tau$, let's write this as:

$$\psi(\tau)=\frac{1}{c}\int_0^\tau f(\tau')d\tau'+K$$

Since we said that $u(\tau=0) = v$, we ought to get: $v=c \tanh(\psi(\tau=0))$. Ie:

$$v=c\tanh\left(\int_0^{\tau=0}f(\tau')d\tau'+K\right)$$ $$=c\tanh(K)$$ $$\implies K=\text{arctanh}\left(\frac{v}{c}\right)$$

Using the identity $\tanh(A+B)=\frac{\tanh A+\tanh B}{1+\tanh A\tanh B}$, we have:

$$u=c\tanh \psi = c \tanh\left(\frac{1}{c}\int_0^{\tau}f(\tau')d\tau'+\text{arctanh} \frac{v}{c}\right)$$

$$=c\frac{\tanh\left(\frac{1}{c}\int_0^{\tau}f(\tau')d\tau'\right)+\tanh \text{arctanh} \frac{v}{c}}{1+\tanh\left(\frac{1}{c}\int_0^{\tau}f(\tau')d\tau'\right)\tanh\text{arctanh} \frac{v}{c}}$$

$$=c\frac{\tanh\left(\frac{1}{c}\int_0^{\tau}f(\tau')d\tau'\right)+\frac{v}{c}}{1+\tanh\left(\frac{1}{c}\int_0^{\tau}f(\tau')d\tau'\right)\frac{v}{c}}$$

$$=\frac{c\tanh\left(\frac{1}{c}\int_0^{\tau}f(\tau')d\tau'\right)+v}{1+\tanh\left(\frac{1}{c}\int_0^{\tau}f(\tau')d\tau'\right)\frac{v}{c}}=u$$

Recall we have: $u'=\frac{u-v}{1-\frac{uv}{c^2}}$ which maps from speeds in $S$ to $S'$. Invert to get a map $S'$ to $S$ (ie change $v$ $-v$, primed to unprimed and vice versa:

$$u=\frac{u'+v}{1+\frac{u'v}{c^2}}$$ $$=\frac{u'+v}{1+\frac{u'}{c}\frac{v}{c}}$$

Compare with what we found:

$$u=\frac{c\tanh\left(\frac{1}{c}\int_0^{\tau}f(\tau')d\tau'\right)+v}{1+\tanh\left(\frac{1}{c}\int_0^{\tau}f(\tau')d\tau'\right)\frac{v}{c}}$$

Conclude:

$$u'=c\tanh\left(\frac{1}{c}\int_0^{\tau}f(\tau')d\tau'\right)$$

Using $S$ to $S'$ map, setting $u_0=v$, writing $u$ as $u(\tau)$, rewrite:

$$\frac{u(\tau)-u_0}{1-\frac{u(\tau)u_0}{c^2}}=c\tanh\left(\frac{1}{c}\int_0^{\tau}f(\tau')d\tau'\right)$$

As required.
